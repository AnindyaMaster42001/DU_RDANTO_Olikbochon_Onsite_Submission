    \begin{abstract}
    \noindent
    We describe our system for the \emph{Olikbochon} Bengali LLM
    Hallucination Detection Challenge, which asks whether a Bengali response
    is faithful or hallucinated, with the heaviest weight on
    Bangladesh-specific (C1) facts. Our central observation is that the task
    splits cleanly into two regimes with opposite failure modes:
    \emph{context-grounded} rows are nearly solved by a substring-overlap rule
    plus a single LLM judge ($\text{F1}\approx0.90$), whereas \emph{no-context}
    closed-book rows collapse to a constant-guess baseline ($\text{F1}=0.35$)
    and carry essentially all of the remaining opportunity. We attack the
    no-context branch with a stacked suite of complementary signals---LLM
    judges at two scales, a cross-lingual consistency check, and, most
    importantly, two \emph{knowledge-grounded} verifiers: a 32B judge that
    reads answers against reranked Bengali-Wikipedia evidence with an
    UNSURE-safe three-way verdict, and a dictionary verifier that grounds
    idiom/word-meaning questions in \emph{bn.wiktionary}---the corpus
    Wikipedia structurally cannot supply. An error analysis shows the field's
    dominant failure is one-sided over-skepticism: true-but-obscure C1 facts
    are systematically flagged as hallucinated. Grounding directly targets
    this bias. Signals are stacked by per-branch nested cross-validation, and
    our honest out-of-fold estimates transfer to the public leaderboard within
    $\sim$0.03. The full system reaches a public leaderboard binary-F1 of
    \textbf{0.815}; a portable seven-signal subset reproduces it within noise
    fully offline inside the Phase-2 compute budget.
    \end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large language models are strikingly fluent in Bengali, a language of over
280 million speakers, and just as frequently and confidently wrong. A model
can name the author of \emph{Bidrohi} correctly in English yet misattribute it
in Bengali; it can narrate the Liberation War in fluent prose with the wrong
dates. These errors do not sound wrong---they sound like Bengali---which is
what makes them dangerous \citep{ji2023survey,guerreiro2023hallucinations}. The
\emph{Olikbochon} challenge (\emph{``the mirage of language''}) frames this as
binary detection: given a prompt, a candidate response, and an optional source
\texttt{context}, predict whether the response is faithful ($\text{label}=1$)
or hallucinated ($\text{label}=0$). The leaderboard scores binary F1 on the
hallucinated class, weighted toward the \textbf{C1} cultural-distance band
where the Bangladeshi answer diverges from a globally dominant default---the
band where the headline phenomenon concentrates.

Our approach rests on a single structural observation and two knowledge-grounding
contributions:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Two-branch decomposition.} Rows with a \texttt{context} are a
\emph{grounding} problem and are already near-solved by cheap signals; rows
without a context are a \emph{closed-book factuality} problem and hold the
entire scoring opportunity (\S\ref{sec:branch}). Treating them separately, with
a per-branch stacker, is worth more than any single model.
\item \textbf{Evidence-grounded judging.} A 32B judge that reads each answer
against \emph{reranked} Bengali-Wikipedia passages, emitting an UNSURE-safe
three-way verdict, is our strongest closed-book signal and rescues obscure
C1 facts that every parametric judge misses (\S\ref{sec:ret32}).
\item \textbf{Dictionary grounding (the ``right book'').} Roughly one in seven
no-context test rows are idiom/word-meaning questions that Wikipedia
\emph{provably} cannot answer---dense retrieval returns grammar and physics
articles. Grounding them in \emph{bn.wiktionary} instead lifts idiom precision
to 0.92 and is applied as a targeted override (\S\ref{sec:wikt}).
\end{itemize}

The result is a calibrated, per-branch stacked ensemble whose honest
out-of-fold (OOF) score tracks the leaderboard tightly and whose portable
subset runs offline within the code-competition limits.

\section{Task and Data}
The organizers release a \textbf{sample split of 299 labeled rows} for
pipeline validation---explicitly not a conventional training set---and a
\textbf{test set of 2{,}516 unlabeled rows}. Each row has \texttt{prompt\_bn},
\texttt{response\_bn}, and a \texttt{context} that is \texttt{[NULL]} for
closed-book items. Of the 2{,}516 test rows, \textbf{1{,}155 ($46\%$) are
no-context}. Cultural-distance bands structure the difficulty: \textbf{C0}
globally stable facts, \textbf{C1} culturally situated (Bangladesh-specific),
and \textbf{C2} contested or time-sensitive facts. Because there is no training
signal beyond 299 examples, the design goal is \emph{generalization}, not
fitting: every modeling decision is judged on a 5-seed $\times$ 5-fold OOF
estimate, and only decisive gains are promoted to a leaderboard check.

Our internal compass is \textbf{macro-F1} on the 299 labeled rows; the official
leaderboard metric is binary F1 on the hallucinated class. We report both and,
crucially, verify that our OOF movements survive the transfer to the hidden
split (\S\ref{sec:results}).

\section{Method}

\subsection{Two-branch decomposition}
\label{sec:branch}
Splitting the 299 samples by presence of \texttt{context} exposes two nearly
disjoint problems. A trivial baseline---substring-overlap grounding for context
rows, constant guessing for the rest---already scores macro-F1 $0.894$ on the
context branch but only $0.345$ on the no-context branch (Table~\ref{tab:prog},
row 1). Context rows are extractive: a short answer either appears in, or
contradicts, the passage, and substring overlap plus one LLM judge captures
almost all of the signal. No-context rows require the model to \emph{know} the
answer. We therefore route every row to a branch and fit a \emph{separate}
stacker per branch, so that a signal earning high weight on closed-book
factuality is never diluted by the context rows it cannot help.

\subsection{Signal suite}
Each signal outputs a per-row $P(\text{faithful})\in[0,1]$.

\paragraph{Cheap grounding signals.} \emph{Substring} overlap between response
and context (the dominant context-branch signal); and an \emph{NLI} check with
mDeBERTa-v3-XNLI \citep{he2021deberta,laurer2024nli} using the context as
premise. NLI \emph{under}-performs substring on the context branch
($0.635$ vs.\ $0.894$)---a short extractive answer is a poor NLI hypothesis---so
it is retained only as a decorrelated minor feature.

\paragraph{LLM judges.} We use Qwen2.5-Instruct \citep{qwen2024} at two scales
as GPTQ-Int4 models via vLLM \citep{kwon2023vllm,frantar2023gptq}. Judges differ
in prompt, quantization, and decoding, which decorrelates their errors: a
14B self-consistency judge; a 14B answer-then-compare self-verifier; a 32B
single-pass judge; and, to counter over-skepticism (\S\ref{sec:analysis}), a
32B \emph{self-verify} judge with a three-way YES/NO/UNSURE head and few-shot
exemplars that include true-but-obscure local facts. Because Bengali is
$\approx$1 token per character in Qwen's vocabulary, all Bengali-heavy prompts
run at \texttt{max\_model\_len}$=8192$.

\paragraph{Cross-lingual consistency.} We ask the model in English and check
agreement with the Bengali answer. Notably, on $\sim$44\% of no-context
questions the model abstains \emph{in English too}: these deep-C1 facts are
beyond parametric recall in any language, which is precisely why external
knowledge is needed.

\subsection{Evidence-grounded 32B judging}
\label{sec:ret32}
Our strongest closed-book signal reads each answer against retrieved evidence
rather than the model's parameters. We build a Bengali-Wikipedia index from the
current \texttt{bnwiki} dump (438{,}788 articles $\rightarrow$ 335{,}628
passages), retrieve with dense \textbf{bge-m3} \citep{chen2024bgem3}, rerank
with \textbf{bge-reranker-v2-m3}, and pass the top passages to the 32B judge.
The judge emits a three-way verdict; critically, \textbf{UNSURE maps to $0.5$}
rather than a wrong NO, so missing evidence abstains instead of manufacturing a
hallucination call. This single signal reaches standalone macro-F1 $0.753$
(previous best $0.688$), is decisive on every row, and rescues 7 of the 23
faithful rows that \emph{every} other signal had missed. It earns the largest
no-context stacker weight in the ensemble.

\subsection{Dictionary grounding: the right book}
\label{sec:wikt}
Error analysis (\S\ref{sec:analysis}) revealed a class of rows no amount of
Wikipedia retrieval can solve: idiom and word-meaning questions. Asked the
figurative sense of \bn{ujaner koi}{an easily obtained thing} or
\bn{dhandha}{a dishonest scheme}, dense retrieval over Wikipedia returns
grammar, physics, and fruit articles---an encyclopedia simply does not contain
idiom glosses. And this is not a corner case: \textbf{162 of the 1{,}155
no-context test rows ($14\%$)} are word/idiom/meaning questions (75 idiom
``bhabartho'' rows, 80 word-meaning, plus synonyms/antonyms/proverbs).

The fix is the correct corpus. We parse \texttt{bn.wiktionary} with a custom
parser (wikiextractor targets article prose, not lexical entries) into
\textbf{73{,}444 entries}---15{,}182 idioms (including 7{,}723 mined from the
\emph{bagdhara} appendix table) and 58{,}262 words---keyed by headword and
definition. Dense bge-m3 retrieval recovers variant spellings that exact lookup
misses, and the 32B judge grounds the given meaning against the retrieved
definition with the same UNSURE-safe protocol.

Accuracy splits sharply by question type. On \textbf{idioms the signal is
$12/13=0.92$}: it passes true idiom glosses and rejects false ones, and grounds
all 75 idiom test rows decisively. On raw word-meaning it is only $0.50$,
wrecked by dataset label noise (definitions that match the dictionary yet are
labeled hallucinated) and polysemy. We therefore deploy it not as a stacker
feature (too sparse to weight) but as a \textbf{targeted idiom-only override}:
where the dictionary verdict is confident on a \emph{bhabartho} row, it
overrides the ensemble. On the test set it flips 26 of 75 idiom predictions
(net $+18$ faithful).

\subsection{Per-branch stacking}
\label{sec:stack}
Signals are combined by \textbf{per-branch logistic regression} refit at run
time on the 299 labeled rows, with weights and decision thresholds estimated by
5-seed $\times$ 5-fold nested cross-validation (the threshold is the CV median).
The stacker allocates the top no-context weight to evidence grounding, with the
32B self-verify and cross-lingual signals contributing complementary weight;
the context branch is carried by substring and the judges. Adding
\emph{more} parametric judges past this point does not help---agreement among
judges is $0.74$--$0.86$ and the ensemble saturates---so gains come only from
new \emph{knowledge} (retrieval, dictionary), not new model scale.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
Table~\ref{tab:prog} traces the honest OOF macro-F1 and the corresponding
public-LB binary-F1 as signals accumulate. The no-context branch moves from a
constant-guess $0.345$ to $\sim$0.78; the context branch stays near $0.90$
throughout. Two facts matter more than any single number. First, the
\textbf{OOF$\rightarrow$LB transfer is tight} ($\approx-0.03$ at every checkpoint):
the 299-row harness is a trustworthy compass despite its size. Second, the last
two gains come entirely from grounding---evidence (ret32) and the dictionary
override---not from stacking additional parametric judges.

The evidence-grounded 32B signal (ret32) lifts OOF from $0.803$ to
$0.833\pm0.008$ and the public LB from $0.759$ to $0.803$. The dictionary
override adds a further $+0.013$ OOF ($0.846\pm0.008$) and $+0.012$ LB
($\rightarrow0.815$), rescuing $\sim$4 of the 16 hardest rows. Pruning two
saturated signals (NLI, cross-lingual) is a further variance-reducing
$+0.005$ OOF hedge.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
\paragraph{The failure mode is one-sided.} On the 167 no-context sample rows,
the 23 rows that \emph{every} signal gets wrong (or abstains on) are
\textbf{all faithful}. Not a single hallucinated row evades all signals. Our
judges are systematically \emph{over-skeptical}: true but obscure C1 answers get
called hallucinated. This one-sided bias---not random error---is the gap to the
top of the leaderboard, and it is exactly what evidence and dictionary
grounding target.

\paragraph{Autopsy of the residual.} After ret32, 16 hard faithful rows remain.
Their taxonomy is instructive: 5 are idiom/proverb rows that defeat every
parametric judge (all $\approx0$) and are solvable \emph{only} by the
wiktionary corpus; 5 are literature/geography/history facts needing deeper
Wikipedia retrieval; 4 are parametric-recall rows the anti-skepticism judges
already read correctly; 1 is an underspecified negation MCQ with its options
stripped; and 1 is \textbf{genuine label noise}---\bn{competent}{unqualified}
is the exact opposite of the truth, so the judges are \emph{correctly} flagging
it. Chasing that last row would mean teaching the model a falsehood, so we
exclude it. A useful byproduct: the dictionary \emph{surfaced} several
mislabeled word-meaning rows, a real ceiling on that slice we reported to the
organizers rather than fit.

\paragraph{Saturation.} Once three grounding-and-judging angles are in the
stack, adding further judges hurts within noise. On a 299-row validation set,
OOF deltas under $\sim$0.02 do not reliably transfer; we treat them as noise and
do not chase them in either direction.

\section{Reproducibility and Phase-2 Package}
The full system uses ten signals, three of which were computed on a personal
workstation. For the offline code-competition run we ship a \textbf{portable
seven-signal subset} that reproduces the result within noise (OOF $0.831$ vs.\
$0.833$; the difference is far below a 299-row resolution) as a single Kaggle
notebook. It recomputes every signal from the raw test CSV for both the 299
labeled rows (to fit the stacker) and the evaluation rows, then writes
\texttt{submission.csv}. Table~\ref{tab:models} lists the weights; all are
open, total $\sim$37\,GB ($<$50\,GB), and GPTQ (not AWQ) quantization is
deliberate so the same weights run on both P100 (sm60) and T4 (sm75). A
verification run on the Phase-1 test set completed offline in \textbf{4.96\,h}
on $2\times$T4---well inside the 9\,h budget---and reproduced our submitted
predictions.

\section{Conclusion}
The Bengali hallucination-detection task is not one problem but two, and its
dominant failure is a one-sided skepticism toward true-but-obscure
culturally-situated facts. Progress came not from larger judges but from giving
the judge the \emph{right evidence}: reranked Wikipedia passages for factual
recall, and---where an encyclopedia is structurally silent---a Wiktionary
idiom dictionary. Both are cheap, offline, and directly aimed at the C1 band the
task rewards. The system reaches a public-LB binary-F1 of 0.815 with an honest
validation harness that transfers to within 0.03 of the leaderboard, and it
reproduces fully offline within the Phase-2 compute budget.
